## 1. What Are Quantum Technologies?

Quantum technologies are not new — they have been part of everyday life for decades. The **first quantum revolution** emerged in the 1920s when quantum mechanics was developed to explain phenomena that classical physics simply could not account for (e.g., black-body radiation, the photoelectric effect).

### First Quantum Revolution — Technologies We Already Use

These technologies passively exploit quantum mechanical properties of materials without directly controlling individual quantum systems:

| Technology | Quantum Basis |
|---|---|
| Semiconductors & transistors | Quantum band theory |
| Lasers | Stimulated emission of photons |
| MRI scanners | Nuclear magnetic resonance |
| Optical fibers | Quantum optics |
| Atomic clocks | Quantum energy level transitions |
| Solar panels | Photoelectric effect |

> **Example:** A second is now defined by the frequency of light emitted by a Caesium-133 atom transitioning between two energy levels — accurate to **1 second in 1.4 million years**.

### Second Quantum Revolution — Active Engineering

The ongoing **second quantum revolution** goes further: it aims to actively manipulate and exploit **individual quantum systems** (single atoms, single photons). This unlocks three major technology families:

- **Quantum Computing** — solving specific problems exponentially faster
- **Quantum Sensing & Metrology** — ultra-precise measurements
- **Quantum Communication** — provably secure key exchange

## 2. Key Quantum Phenomena

Two quantum phenomena are central to understanding quantum computing and cryptography:

### Superposition

A quantum system (e.g., a qubit) can exist in **multiple states simultaneously** until it is measured. The act of measurement forces it to "collapse" into one definite state.

The classic thought experiment is **Schrödinger's Cat** — a cat in a box that is simultaneously alive and dead until observed.

In practice: a **qubit** can be in a superposition of `|0⟩` and `|1⟩`, unlike a classical bit which is strictly 0 or 1.

### Entanglement

Two or more quantum particles can become **entangled** — their quantum states are correlated regardless of the distance between them. Measuring one particle instantly determines the state of its entangled partner.

This is not faster-than-light communication, but it is a powerful resource for quantum cryptography and quantum computing.

### Why These Matter for Computing

- **Superposition** allows quantum computers to process many possible inputs simultaneously
- **Entanglement** enables correlations between qubits that have no classical equivalent
- Together, they allow certain algorithms to reach the answer in **far fewer steps** than any classical algorithm

## 3. Quantum Computing

### From Bits to Qubits

| Property | Classical Bit | Qubit |
|---|---|---|
| States | 0 or 1 | `|0⟩`, `|1⟩`, or any superposition |
| Measurement | Reads exact value | Collapses superposition probabilistically |
| Multi-bit state | 2ⁿ states (one at a time) | 2ⁿ amplitudes (simultaneously) |
| Entanglement | Not possible | Possible |

### Physical Implementations

Building qubits is hard — they must be isolated from the environment (to stay quantum) while still being controllable. Current approaches include:

- **Superconducting qubits** — IBM, Google (require cooling to ~15 millikelvin, colder than outer space)
- **Trapped ion qubits** — Quantinuum, IonQ (very accurate but slow)
- **Photonic qubits** — PsiQuantum
- **Topological qubits** — Microsoft (Majorana-based, still early stage)

### Current State: NISQ Era

Today's quantum computers are called **NISQ** — *Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum*. Key facts:

- IBM's publicly available systems have **hundreds of noisy physical qubits**
- You can try quantum computing today for free via [IBM Quantum Platform](https://quantum.cloud.ibm.com/)
- Google demonstrated in December 2024 that **surface codes** can produce "better" logical qubits (error correction below threshold)
- The next major milestone is a **fault-tolerant qubit** with gate error rates ~10⁻⁶

### What Can Quantum Computers Actually Do Better?

Quantum computers are **not general-purpose speed-ups**. They only outperform classical computers for specific problems:

| Problem Type | Quantum Algorithm | Speedup | Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Integer factorization | Shor's (1994) | **Exponential** | Breaks RSA, DH, ECC |
| Discrete logarithm | Shor's (1994) | **Exponential** | Breaks DH, ECDH |
| Unstructured search | Grover's (1996) | Quadratic (√N) | Weakens symmetric crypto |
| Quantum simulation | Various | Exponential | Drug discovery, materials |
| Linear systems | HHL algorithm | Exponential* | Finance, ML |

> *Note: Many claimed quantum speedups have been "ewinized" (classically matched) — finding genuinely useful quantum algorithms is harder than it looks.

Quantum computers will **complement** classical computers as an acceleration platform (similar to how GPUs complement CPUs), not replace them.

## 4. The Quantum Threat to Security

This is the critical section for IT and security professionals.

### The Core Problem

Modern **public-key cryptography** derives its security from the computational hardness of mathematical problems:

- **RSA** → security relies on the hardness of **integer factorization**
- **Diffie-Hellman (DH)** → security relies on the hardness of the **discrete logarithm problem**
- **Elliptic Curve (ECDH, ECDSA)** → security relies on the **elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem**

**Shor's algorithm solves both factorization and discrete logarithm problems in polynomial time** on a sufficiently large quantum computer.

This means a cryptographically relevant quantum computer could:

- **Break encrypted communications** (confidentiality) — decrypt TLS sessions, VPNs, email
- **Forge digital signatures** (integrity/authentication) — impersonate websites, software publishers, users
- **Compromise PKI** — forge certificates, undermine the entire certificate authority chain

### What Is NOT Broken by Quantum Computers

| Cryptographic Primitive | Quantum Impact | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| AES-128 encryption | **Weakened** (→ use AES-256) | Grover halves key space effectively |
| AES-256 encryption | **Safe** | 128-bit security remains after Grover |
| SHA-256 / SHA-3 hashing | **Mostly safe** | Grover has limited practical impact |
| One-time pad | **Safe** | Information-theoretically secure |
| RSA (any key size) | **Broken** | Shor's algorithm |
| Diffie-Hellman | **Broken** | Shor's algorithm |
| ECDH / ECDSA | **Broken** | Shor's algorithm |

### The "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" Attack

This is the **most urgent threat today**, even before any large quantum computer exists:

```
Attacker captures encrypted traffic NOW (2025)
         ↓
Stores it in cold storage
         ↓
Waits until quantum computer is available (202X)
         ↓
Decrypts all historical traffic retrospectively
```

**Any data that must remain confidential for 10+ years is already at risk.** This includes:

- Classified government communications
- Healthcare records
- Financial transaction histories
- Intellectual property
- Long-term contracts and legal documents

### Mosca's Theorem

The framework for deciding **when you need to act** is Mosca's Theorem:

```
If:
  (migration time to quantum-safe crypto) 
  + (required data confidentiality period) 
  > (time until cryptographically relevant quantum computer)

Then: You have a problem.
```

Concrete examples of how "relevant time" varies:

| Use Case | Data Confidentiality Period | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| Quick chat message | Hours | Low |
| E-banking session authentication | Days | Low-Medium |
| Medical records | 30+ years | **High** |
| Mortgage signature | 25+ years | **High** |
| Government secrets | 50+ years | **Critical** |
| Industrial control systems | Lifetime of hardware | **Critical** |

### Timeline Uncertainty

No one knows exactly when a cryptographically relevant quantum computer will exist. Estimates vary widely:

- Breaking RSA-2048 requires approximately **20 million physical qubits** (per 2021 estimates)
- Improvements in quantum error correction have reduced this estimate by **20× in recent years**
- Current best machines: ~1,000 noisy physical qubits
- The gap is still enormous — but it is closing

**The uncertainty itself is the reason to act now.**

## 6. Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC)

Post-quantum cryptography is **classical (software-based) cryptography** designed to resist attacks from both classical and quantum computers. It replaces vulnerable public-key algorithms with new ones based on different hard mathematical problems.

### NIST Standardization Process

NIST launched a global competition in 2016 to standardize quantum-resistant algorithms. The process involved hundreds of submissions and multiple rounds of analysis.

**Finalized Standards (2024):**

| Standard | Algorithm | Type | Mathematical Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| FIPS 203 | **ML-KEM** (Kyber) | Key Encapsulation | Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) |
| FIPS 204 | **ML-DSA** (Dilithium) | Digital Signature | Module Learning With Errors |
| FIPS 205 | **SLH-DSA** (SPHINCS+) | Digital Signature | Hash functions only |

**Coming Soon:**
- FIPS 206: **FN-DSA** (FALCON) — lattice-based signatures, very compact
- FIPS ???: Based on **HQC** — code-based key encapsulation (backup for ML-KEM)

### Why Multiple Algorithms?

Diversity is intentional — if one mathematical assumption is broken (e.g., a new algorithm for lattice problems), alternatives remain secure.

### PQC vs. Classical Crypto — Performance Comparison

| Algorithm | Public Key | Signature | Sign Speed | Verify Speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ed25519 (classical) | 44 B | 64 B | 269 µs | 805 µs |
| RSA-2048 (classical) | 272 B | 256 B | 803 µs | 0.4 µs |
| **ML-DSA-65 (PQC)** | 1,952 B | 3,309 B | 97 µs | 46 µs |
| SLH-DSA-128s (PQC) | 32 B | 7,856 B | 14,000 µs | 40 µs |
| FN-DSA-512 (PQC) | 897 B | 666 B | 3 µs | 0.7 µs |

**Key takeaway:** PQC signatures are significantly larger, which matters for TLS certificates, code signing, and PKI infrastructure.

### Migration Is Happening NOW

The PQC migration is not waiting for quantum computers — it is already underway:

- **~56% of HTTPS traffic** is already protected with quantum-safe key exchange (hybrid ML-KEM + X25519), as of late 2025 (Cloudflare data)
- **Firefox, Chrome** support hybrid PQC key exchange
- **Signal** deployed post-quantum ratchets (PQXDH)
- **OpenSSH 9.0+** supports ML-KEM by default (`mlkem768x25519-sha256`)
- **OpenSSL 3.5** includes native PQC support (ML-KEM, ML-DSA, SLH-DSA)
- **AWS, Google, Zoom** have rolled out PQC key encapsulation

### The Two Migrations

**Migration 1 — Key Exchange (mostly done):**
- Replacing ECDH/DH with ML-KEM
- Relatively easy: drop-in replacement, performance overhead acceptable
- Already deployed across most major internet infrastructure

**Migration 2 — Digital Signatures (harder, ~2026–2030):**
- Replacing RSA/ECDSA in TLS certificates, code signing, PKI
- Much harder: involves CAs, HSMs, CA/B Forum, certificate lifetime policies, legacy devices
- ~0% of the internet uses quantum-safe authentication today
- First PQ certificates expected during 2026
- Default adoption of PQ certificates estimated ~2030

> **For IT teams:** Start your cryptographic inventory now. Identify all uses of RSA, ECDH, and ECDSA in your systems — certificates, SSH keys, JWT signing, VPN configs, hardware security modules.

## 7. Quantum Cryptography

Quantum cryptography is **fundamentally different** from post-quantum cryptography, despite the similar names. It uses the laws of physics — not mathematical hardness — to provide security.

### Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)

QKD allows two parties (Alice and Bob) to generate a shared secret key over an untrusted quantum channel, with **information-theoretic security** — meaning security that holds even against an adversary with unlimited computational power (including quantum computers).

#### The Physical Principle

QKD typically uses **photon polarization**. A photon sent through a polarizing filter will either pass through or be blocked depending on the alignment:

- `0°` filter → horizontally polarized photon passes through
- `90°` filter → horizontally polarized photon is blocked
- `45°` filter → **50% chance** either way (the photon's state is disturbed)

The key insight: **measuring a quantum state disturbs it**. An eavesdropper (Eve) cannot intercept and re-transmit photons without introducing detectable errors.

#### BB84 Protocol (Step by Step)

BB84 is the foundational QKD protocol, invented by Bennett and Brassard in 1984:

```
Step 1: Quantum transmission
  Alice sends photons with random polarizations
  encoding random bits in two bases (+ or ×)

Step 2: Measurement  
  Bob measures each photon using a randomly chosen basis
  (~50% of measurements use the wrong basis → random result)

Step 3: Sifting (over classical authenticated channel)
  Alice and Bob publicly compare which bases they used
  Bits where bases don't match → discarded
  Remaining bits form the "sifted key"

Step 4: Parameter estimation
  They compare a small sample of the sifted key
  If error rate too high → Eve is present → ABORT
  If error rate acceptable → proceed

Step 5: Information reconciliation
  Correct remaining errors using error-correcting codes
  (some information about the key is leaked to Eve)

Step 6: Privacy amplification
  Apply a hash function to shorten the key
  This "amplifies" security, removing Eve's partial knowledge
  Result: shorter but perfectly secret key
```

#### What QKD Provides (and Doesn't)

| Property | QKD | PQC |
|---|---|---|
| Security assumption | Laws of physics | Mathematical hardness |
| Secure against quantum computers | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Requires quantum hardware | ✅ Yes | ❌ No (software only) |
| Works over long distances | ❌ Limited (~300 km fiber) | ✅ Yes (internet) |
| Key rate (speed) | Low (kbps–Mbps) | High |
| Authentication requirement | Needs initial shared secret or PQC auth | Self-contained |
| Cost | ~CHF 100,000 per device pair | Minimal |

#### Hardware Requirements for QKD

**Needed:**
- Direct optical fiber connection (or free-space optical link/satellite)
- Single-photon laser source
- Beam splitter
- Single-photon detector

**NOT needed:**
- Quantum computer
- Quantum memory
- Complex multi-particle interactions

Commercial QKD devices are available today from companies like **ID Quantique** (Swiss), **Toshiba**, **Huawei**. A full QKD appliance pair costs approximately **CHF 100,000**.

### Quantum Random Number Generators (QRNG)

Classical computers cannot produce truly random numbers — only pseudo-random numbers generated by deterministic algorithms. True randomness requires a quantum source.

**How QRNGs work:** Send photons through a 50/50 beam splitter. Quantum mechanics guarantees each photon has exactly 50% probability of going each way — this is provably non-deterministic, not just apparently random.

**Use cases:**
- Seeding cryptographic key generation
- Generating one-time pads
- Improving security of any randomness-dependent protocol

Commercial QRNGs are much cheaper than QKD devices — around **CHF 1,000**, and some modern smartphones include QRNG chips (Samsung Galaxy series).

### QKD in Networks

Point-to-point QKD is limited to ~300 km due to photon loss in fiber. Extending range requires:

- **Trusted relays** — intermediate nodes that re-key (but must be physically secured)
- **Satellite QKD** — China's MICIUS satellite demonstrated intercontinental QKD
- **Quantum repeaters** — theoretical devices requiring quantum memory (not yet practical)
- **European Quantum Internet** — the European Commission and ESA are building a pan-European quantum communication infrastructure

### QKD vs. PQC — Not a Competition

A common misconception is that QKD and PQC compete with each other. In fact:

- **QKD needs PQC** — QKD requires an authenticated classical channel, which requires digital signatures, which requires PQC (or a pre-shared secret)
- **PQC is the foundation** — PQC migration is happening regardless of QKD
- **QKD adds a layer** — for adversaries who don't trust any mathematical assumption, QKD provides physics-based guarantees
- **The question is not "which one"** — it's "which combination makes sense for your threat model and budget"

**Recommended transition roadmap (HSLU):**

```
Phase 1 (Now):    Deploy Quantum Random Number Generators (QRNG)
Phase 2 (Now):    Migrate to Post-Quantum Algorithms (PQC)
Phase 3 (Future): Deploy Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) where justified
```

## 8. When Should You Act?

### Security Level Comparison (Post-Quantum vs. Classical)

| Application | Current Algorithm | Recommended Action |
|---|---|---|
| HTTPS/TLS key exchange | ECDH (P-256) | ✅ Migrate to ML-KEM now |
| TLS certificates | RSA-2048 / ECDSA | ⏳ Prepare for ML-DSA (~2026) |
| SSH keys | RSA / Ed25519 | ⏳ Evaluate hybrid schemes |
| Code signing | RSA / ECDSA | ⏳ Plan migration |
| VPN (IKE/IPsec) | DH / ECDH | 🔴 Assess urgency by data lifetime |
| Long-term document signing | RSA | 🔴 High urgency |
| Encrypted archives (long retention) | RSA key wrap | 🔴 Immediate action needed |

### Regulatory Pressure

Several bodies are already mandating PQC migration:

- **NIST** — finalized standards; US agencies must migrate
- **BSI (Germany)** — published PQC recommendations, timeline guidance
- **NCSC (UK)** — migration guidance published
- **Financial regulators** — increasing pressure on banks and payment systems
- **Healthcare** — long data retention requirements make this urgent

### Practical Steps for IT Teams

1. **Cryptographic inventory** — catalog all algorithms, key sizes, certificate expiries across your infrastructure
2. **Prioritize by data sensitivity and longevity** — what needs to stay secret for how long?
3. **Deploy hybrid schemes** — combine classical + PQC (e.g., X25519 + ML-KEM) during transition for compatibility
4. **Update libraries** — OpenSSL 3.5+, liboqs (Open Quantum Safe project)
5. **Monitor standards** — NIST continues releasing new standards; stay current
6. **Test performance** — PQC signatures are larger; assess impact on TLS handshakes, certificate chains, HSM throughput
7. **Plan for crypto-agility** — design systems to swap algorithms without re-architecting

## 9. Key Takeaways

### Quantum Computing

- Quantum computers exploit superposition and entanglement to solve **specific problems** much faster
- Current machines (NISQ era) are **not yet cryptographically threatening** — too noisy, too few qubits
- **Shor's algorithm** will break all RSA, DH, and elliptic curve cryptography on a sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer
- Timeline is uncertain (likely 10–20 years), but "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks are happening **today**

### Post-Quantum Cryptography

- PQC is **classical math-based software** — no quantum hardware required
- NIST standards exist: **ML-KEM** (key exchange), **ML-DSA** and **SLH-DSA** (signatures)
- **~56% of internet traffic** already uses quantum-safe key exchange
- Digital signatures migration is harder and will take until ~2030
- **Start your cryptographic inventory and migration planning now**

### Quantum Cryptography

- Provides **information-theoretic security** based on physics, not math
- **QKD** generates provably secret keys — secure even against quantum computers
- **QRNG** generates provably true randomness
- Requires specialized hardware and direct fiber connections
- **QKD needs PQC** to work in practice — they are complementary, not competing
- PQC adoption is certain; QKD adoption depends on specific high-security use cases
